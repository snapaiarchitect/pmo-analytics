💡 DoD = 65.4% of all contract value.Agency concentration is extreme — the top agency alone accounts for nearly two-thirds of the portfolio.
💡 Vendor market is unconcentrated.HHI of 439 means many vendors share the market. Lockheed Martin leads at 14.1% but no single vendor dominates.
💡 Multi-decade contract ceilings span 1978–2100.These are maximum potential obligations, not annual spending. The timeline shows procurement cycles, not cash flow.
💡 PSC codes reveal procurement strategy.Product vs service mix varies by agency. Understanding PSC distribution helps predict contract vehicle preferences.
💡 Virginia leads geographic distribution.Proximity to Pentagon and defense contractors drives state-level concentration. Not all spending is DC-centric.
💡 NAICS distribution shows industrial footprint.Manufacturing and professional services dominate. This drives vendor diversity and competition rate expectations.
💡 Heavy Rail = $5.3B (39.6% of capital).Rail modes combined capture 79.5% of all FTA capital. Bus gets 16% — the rest is scattered across 16 other modes.
💡 Top 5 states capture nearly half of capital.Large urban agencies concentrate investment geographically. New York, California, Illinois lead.
💡 Commuter Rail costs most per vehicle.Mode-specific cost analysis reveals where capital efficiency differs most. Heavy rail has high absolute cost but also high ridership.
💡 Rehab dominates over new expansion.Most agencies spend on maintaining existing infrastructure rather than building new. This signals a mature transit ecosystem.
💡 MTA New York leads at $4.4B.The top agency alone accounts for a significant share of all capital. Agency-level concentration is the real story.
💡 Capital intensity varies by urban area.Per-capita and per-mile capital reveal which markets are over- or under-invested relative to their scale.
💡 Peak unemployment: 11.3% (April 2020).The COVID shock is visible as a dramatic spike. Recovery has been gradual but incomplete — latest is 5.6%.
💡 Employment dropped 77.2K in one month (Mar–Apr 2020).The single-month loss is the steepest in the dataset. Recovery has been slow — still below Jan 2020 baseline.
💡 Unemployment and employment move inversely but not symmetrically.The dual-axis view shows the labor market's structural asymmetry — jobs recover slower than unemployment falls.
💡 Year-over-year changes show cyclical patterns.Annual comparisons strip out seasonality and reveal the true recovery trajectory since 2020.
💡 Indexed to Jan 2020 = 100.Employment is still below baseline. The recovery line shows how far the labor market has to go to reach pre-COVID levels.
💡 April 2020 was the inflection point.The shock analysis isolates the exact month where the labor market broke — and how long the after-effects persisted.
💡 Annual averages smooth the monthly noise.2020 averaged 8.9% — the worst year. 2022 was 4.8%, the best. The trend is improving but fragile.
💡 DC population: 670,587 (Census ACS 2023).Demographic snapshot cards give executives the key numbers at a glance — population, income, education, poverty.
💡 DC median income is 26.2% above national average.At $101,722, DC outperforms the US median of $80,610. But poverty is also higher — the gap matters.
💡 25.8% have Bachelor's+ but 15.1% live in poverty.The education-poverty paradox shows that high human capital doesn't eliminate poverty — cost of living is the missing variable.
💡 5 of 10 agencies score below target (70).The CTO office leads at 85. Employment Services is lowest at 55. The spread shows governance inconsistency.
💡 Budget vs performance scatter reveals outliers.Some agencies spend more and perform worse — the efficiency frontier shows where reallocation could help.
💡 Executive scorecard aggregates 4 dimensions.Labor, demographics, agency performance, and scenario status in one view for leadership briefings.
💡 Three scenarios: baseline, optimistic, pessimistic.What-if budget reallocation shows how shifting 10% across agencies changes outcomes. Optimistic scenario requires political capital.
💡 Waterfall shows budget change by agency.Positive deltas = more funding. Negative = cuts. The cascade reveals which agencies absorb the pain in each scenario.
💡 6 KPIs for leadership consumption.Unemployment rate, employment level, median income, poverty rate, agency average, and scenario delta — all on one screen.
💡 Sensitivity analysis shows which inputs matter most.Small changes in unemployment assumptions produce large output swings — that's where the risk lives.
💡 Risk classes: low, medium, high, critical.Distribution shows most contracts cluster in medium risk — but the tail of high-risk contracts is where problems concentrate.
💡 Risk scores follow a right-skewed distribution.Most contracts score moderate but a meaningful subset (395 of 1,000) show elevated risk requiring attention.
💡 Duration vs risk score shows structural pattern.Longer contracts aren't always riskier — it's the interaction between duration, agency, and vendor experience that predicts delay.
💡 Schedule variance is the leading indicator.Where planned vs actual duration diverges early, the contract is likely to extend further. Early flags save money.
💡 Critical path analysis identifies bottleneck dependencies.Contracts with more interdependencies have higher delay probability. Simpler procurement paths finish faster.
💡 Monte Carlo simulates 10,000 possible outcomes.The simulation range shows the full uncertainty distribution — not just a single point estimate of completion date.
💡 Delay probability curves by contract age.Older contracts have higher delay likelihood — but not linearly. There's a sweet spot where risk peaks and then stabilizes.
💡 Earned value metrics track program health.CPI and SPI together show whether a contract is over budget, behind schedule, or both. The quadrant matters.
💡 Summary view for portfolio-level risk.Aggregating across all 1,000 contracts shows the portfolio's overall delay exposure and where to focus attention.
💡 Risk matrix maps probability vs impact.High-probability + high-impact contracts go in the red zone. That's the portfolio's danger quadrant.
💡 Portfolio risk summary for executives.One chart tells the story: X% low risk, Y% medium, Z% high. The color-coded bands make it instantly readable.
💡 State-level program summary across all projects.The top-level view shows which states are active in the portfolio and where the investment concentration lies.
💡 Top agencies by program volume and value.Agency rankings change when ranked by count vs. dollars — the dual view prevents misallocation.
💡 Year-over-year trend shows portfolio momentum.Growth, flat, or declining — the direction matters more than the absolute value for strategic planning.
💡 Urban vs rural distribution reveals geographic equity.Some programs concentrate in metro areas while others serve rural communities. The split affects accessibility.
💡 Benchmarking compares agencies on common metrics.Standardized scores let you compare apples to apples across agencies with different sizes and missions.
💡 Focus areas show what each agency prioritizes.Some agencies spread across many programs; others concentrate. Both strategies have trade-offs.
💡 Vehicle-level cost analysis for fleet programs.Per-unit costs reveal economies of scale and outliers. High-cost units may need investigation or justification.
💡 Category trends show which sectors are growing.Program category breakdowns over time reveal where federal investment is shifting — and where it's not.
💡 Fleet modernization tracks replacement cycles.Age distributions and replacement schedules show whether the fleet is modernizing or aging in place.
💡 Metro-area time series for urban program tracking.City-level trends show where programs are expanding or contracting geographically over time.
💡 State heatmap shows geographic intensity at a glance.Color density reveals where programs cluster — useful for resource allocation and travel planning.
💡 Scatter plots reveal state-level correlations.Two-dimensional views of program metrics show which states are outliers — high performance, low investment, or vice versa.
💡 Treemap shows proportional state contribution.Area = value. The biggest rectangles are the states that drive the portfolio's total value.
